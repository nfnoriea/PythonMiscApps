DESCRIPTION OF PROJECT
Provide a brief, non-technical description and purpose of the project.
The McFrederick lab studies interactions between microbes that associate with both wild and domesticated bees. For example, we routinely isolate bacteria and fungi from bee nests that have been collected in the field. We use these isolated Bacteria and Fungi to conduct Bioassays with the bee hosts to determine whether interactions between fungi, bacteria and the host influence host development, growth and reproduction. For example, we isolate bacteria in the genera Lactobacillus, Fructobacillus, and Saccharibacter and saprophytic fungi in the genera Cladosporium, Alternaria, Aspergillus and Pleospora. We have purchased/been given bees from across state lines, e.g. Idaho and Utah. However, the movement of these bees (Megachile rotundata) is not regulated by the USDA or CDFA, and therefore no permits are required for this transport. We also work with bee-specific viruses, such as Deformed Wing Virus and Israeli Acute Paralysis Virus. These are common viruses in California bee populations, so we will not impact wild or agriculturally important bee populations by isolating these viruses. Finally, we work with a fungal pathogen (Ascosphaera aggregata) that is specific to the alfalfa leafcutter bee Megachile rotundata. We monitor the growth of this pathogen in the presence of other fungi and bacteria, in order to determine whether specific bacteria or fungi can inhibit the pathogen's growth.
What are the specific objectives of the work with biohazardous materials?
The specific objectives of our work are to understand how bee-associated microbes are spread, how these microbes affect bee health, and ultimately, whether we can increase the availability of beneficial microbes such as Lactobacilli to managed and wild bee populations. We therefore view the goal of our worjk as conservation of pollinating insect populations through the study of symbiosis.
