DESCRIPTION OF PROJECT
Provide a brief, non-technical description and purpose of the project.
The McCole Lab aims to understand the effects of inflammation on epithelial barrier function incorporating electrophysiology, molecular biology, biochemical and imaging approaches both in vitro and in vivo model systems. We also translate our studies to human disease using intestinal biopsies from IBD patients. We will be obtaining intestinal biopsies from Riverside County Regional Medical Center through a collaboration with a gastroenterologist, Dr. Henry Nguyen. In addition, we are investigating novel treatments to restore barrier function. We will also seek to determine if various strains of intestinal commensal bacteria can infect mouse and human cell lines, and whether these bacteria influence mice and/or human cells by performing gene expression and protein analyses. One method involves studying movement of bacterial molecules into host cells using approaches such as use of methionine analogs AHA and ANL, the latter of which require bacteria be transformed with the pAM1 plasmid (which contains a modified version of the methionyl-tRNA synthetase gene). With a few exceptions being BSL2 bacterial strains, most bacterial and eukaryotic cell lines have been classified as BSL1 by ATCC. Intestinal commensal bacteria include but are not limited to: Clostridium ramosum, Bacteroides vulgatus, Clostridium innocuum, Lactobacillus johnsonii, Lactobacillus animalis, Escherichia coli, Enterococcus faecium.
What are the specific objectives of the work with biohazardous materials?
To understand and evaluate epithelial barrier function we utilize transgenic mice models mimicking diseased states of inflammation. Biohazardous reagents such as phenol-chloroform, Dextran sodium sulfate, EtBr, paraformaldehyde, viral constructs, etc., will be utilized to carry out the molecular techniques necessary to achieve this objective. To address the importance of the microbiome on the epithelial barrier function, we will also infect cells and live mice with infectious and commensal bacteria. Additionally, we will handle defibrinated sheep blood to prepare Brucella Blood Agar Plates (BBA) for isolation and cultivation of nonfastidious and fastidious microorganisms from a variety of clinical and nonclinical specimens. All personnel will be trained accordingly and appropriate measures taken to prevent injury as well as spread of infectious agents. SOP's for the lab have been drafted and will be available as a reference.
