DESCRIPTION OF PROJECT
Provide a brief, non-technical description and purpose of the project.
In the United States, Cucurbit yellow stunting disorder virus (CYSDV) is rampant in melon-production fields in the Southwest,including California and Arizona. Based on comparative coat protein nucleotide sequence analyses, CYSDV isolates can be grouped into two diverged subpopulations: an Eastern subpopulation, and a Western subpopulation. The latter, to which the Californian CYSDV isolates belong, is the most prevalent in the United States. One of the goals of this project is to help the industry identify the diversity of these isolates, and this will be determined using high-throughput RNA-seq Illumina sequencing technology. The project also aims to develop the cloned infectious cDNAs of one or more of these CYSDV isolates to be used in conjunction with agroinoculation for the mass screening of cucurbit hosts for resistance against the virus.
What are the specific objectives of the work with biohazardous materials?
The specific objectives of the work are: 1. Sample CYSDV isolates from Cucurbit crops in California, preferably cucumber. 2. Generation of full-length CYSDV cDNA clones and the re-sequencing of these clones. 3. Select cDNA clones to be used for engineering the full-length cDNAs (corresponding to the viral genomic RNAs) in binary vectors. 4. Perform experiments to determine if the full-length cDNA clones are capable of initiating infection in plants (Nicotiana benthamiana, cucumber, melon and squash) through agro-inoculation procedures.

If “yes”, describe any potential environmental or health hazards, containment precautions, and disposal protocols. Plants infected with CYSDV by agroinoculation are placed in a growth room (Webber 3206D) for three weeks following agroinoculation. There are no whitefly vectors in the room. After they have been incubator for three weeks, the plants are transported by covered rubbermaid containers to and housed in a greenhouse (GH14E) fitted with insect screens to prevent entry or escape of whitefly (Bemisia tabaci) vector of the virus. Note that no whiteflies will be used for this project. The screened greenhouse is to ensure that no whiteflies enter accidentally; and if they do, that they are contained. To further ensure stringent containment of viruses, the greenhouse is on a regular pesticide spray schedule. When analyses of plants are completed, all infected plants will be autoclaved.
