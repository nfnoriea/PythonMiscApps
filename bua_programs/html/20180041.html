DESCRIPTION OF PROJECT
Provide a brief, non-technical description and purpose of the project.
My research program is focused on understanding the mechanisms by which bacterial and oomycete pathogens facilitate infection of their plant hosts. The major focus in my lab is to use effector proteins produced by the plant pathogens Candidatus Liberibacter (a bacterium) and Phytophthora species (a group of oomycete, filamentous eukaryotic pathogens) to study genes in pathogens that are involved in promoting infection of plant hosts, such as citrus, soybean, tobacco and Arabidopsis. An integrative approach using a combination of genomic, genetic, molecular, biochemical and cell biology methods is employed to address fundamental questions including what are the key virulence factors that contribute to plant disease; what are their roles during infection; and how have they evolved during the arms race with plant defense mechanisms. Our work on the citrus bacterial pathogens Candidatus Liberibacter also aims to develop diagnostic methods for citrus Huanglongbing (HLB). This project will significantly advance our understanding of the molecular basis of plant-microbe interactions and contribute to the development of novel, effective and sustainable disease management strategies.
What are the specific objectives of the work with biohazardous materials?
To study the functions of pathogen effectors, we will generate transgenic bacteria, Phytophthora, and plants (mainly Arabidopsis) that either over-expressing specific effector genes or with reduced expression of these effectors. Specific experimental procedures include standard molecular cloning techniques, biochemical analysis and plant infection assays. The molecular cloning procedures include: genomic DNA isolation; PCR; gene cloning in plasmid vectors replicating in E. coli, yeast and plants (Arabidopsis, citrus, soybean and tobacco); constructing mutant strains by over-expressing genes of interest or knocking out genes of interest in Phytophthora species. Plant (Arabidopsis, soybean and tobacco) infection assays involve inoculating plant leaves or roots with bacterial cell or Phytophthora zoospore suspensions, followed by the evaluation of disease progression at 3-6 days after inoculation. These are all standard protocols that are well-established and have been used in numerous labs worldwide. We are planning to maintain citrus seedlings infected with Candidatus Liberibacter in a BSL3 facility that will become available to research in Fall 2018.

If “yes” please describe any potential impact on the environment and the precautions taken to prevent release. All the microorganisms in my laboratory are handled with care in the biosafety cabinets (BSL-2). Plants infected with Pseudomonas syringae or Phytophthora species will be contained in domes in growth facilities (growth chambers or growth rooms) in the Genomics building with lockable doors. The infected plants (including soil) will be autoclaved before disposal. The pots, trays and domes are soaked in 10% bleach first and then washed by detergent.
Is your plant interacting with a recombinant DNA-modified restricted/exotic organism? True
If “yes” please describe the organism. Once a pathogen gene involved in disease development is identified, the gene function will be studied by plant infection assays using genetically modified pathogen or transgenic plants expressing the pathogen genes. The genetically modified pathogen strains include those with the gene of interest either knocked out or over-expressed in a "wild-type" strain. These mutants and the infected plants will be handled with care in the same way as described above. To generate plants expressing the pathogen genes, Agrobacterium tumefaciens carrying the genes of interest will be used to introduce the genes into Nicotiana benthamiana or Arabidopsis. Disease symptoms and plant defense response will be examined in the transgenic plants. The seeds of the transgenic plants will be securely stored and the infected plants will be autoclaved after each experiment.
