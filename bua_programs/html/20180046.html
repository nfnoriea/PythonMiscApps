DESCRIPTION OF PROJECT
Provide a brief, non-technical description and purpose of the project.
We are interested in molecular, sensory and circuit functions of the insect taste system and its interactions with behavior and physiology. The taste system detects non-volatile environmental chemicals that signal the presence of food, toxins, mating partners, or egg-laying sites. Chemicals are detected by membrane proteins encoded by members of various receptor families (Gustatory receptor (Gr), Inotropic receptor (Ir), Pickpocket (Ppk), Transient Receptor Potential (Trp), Odorant receptor (Or)), which are expressed in dendrites of neurons located in taste organs. Axons of chemosensory neurons project to regions in the brain and thoracic ganglia, where they make their first synaptic connections. We will address some fundamental problems in insect chemosensory systems and neuroscience using Drosophila melanogaster and three mosquito species, Anopheles gambiae, Aedes aegypti, Culex quinquefasciatus, as experimental models. Specifically, we will investigate 1) how taste receptors recognize specific compounds, 2) how sensory neurons respond to specific chemicals, 3) how sensory neurons connect with higher order neurons at an anatomical and functional level, 4) how activation of taste circuits gives rise to specific behaviors and physiological outcomes, and 5) how taste circuits and behaviors are modulated by factors such as experience, context and internal state. We will also use various Drosophila species to study the evolution of taste receptor genes and their roles in determining variation in feeding preference across species. We will address these questions in insect models with a multidisciplinary approach using molecular, genetic, electrophysiological, behavioral and imaging techniques. Transgenic approaches will be restricted to the genetic model Drosophila melanogaster, the common housefly Musca domestica, and Aedes aegypti mosquitoes. For functional studies in flies and mosquitoes we will use RNAi and genome-editing strategies such as CRISPR. We will apply the principles gleaned from studies with Drosophila to insects of commercial and health relevance, including the named mosquito disease vectors and nuisance pest Musca domestica. We are also interested in developing two models: a Drosophila in vivo functional expression system to characterize taste receptors from vertebrates including humans, and a genetically engineered housefly to search and seek targeted chemicals in the environment. Transgenic mosquitoes will be reared in the INQ (Room 156). Batches of mosquitoes will be transported to Genomics in vials or cages held in secondary containers held in cardboard boxes labeled with biohazard symbols, and maintained in Room 2210. Transgenic houseflies will be reared, maintained and tested for behavior in Genomics 2210. This room has a screen at the entrance and an electric racquet to kill escapees. Vials with one or few insects will be carried in secondary containers to Room 2223A or 2207B for imaging or electrophysiology experiments.

1. Use of standard recombinant DNA technology to engineer plasmids containing DNA fragments of interest. 2. Use of standard methods to generate transgenic insects (Drosophila melanogaster, Aedes aegypti, Musca domestica) for laboratory experiments.
