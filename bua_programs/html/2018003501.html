DESCRIPTION OF PROJECT
Provide a brief, non-technical description and purpose of the project.
The amendment notes the relocation of mosquitoes from 135 to 236 and 238 and the use of 234 for whiteflies. We undertake research aimed at developing genetic and chemical based insect pest control strategies for medically and agriculturally important insect species. The mosquito species we focus on are the yellow fever mosquito Aedes aegypti, the southern house mosquito, Culex pipiens quinquefasciatus, the African malaria mosquito Anopheles coluzzii, and the southern asian malaria mosquito Anopheles stephensi. Our approaches are to develop gene tagging strategies that identify genes expressed in tissues involved in pathogen transmission and to explore how a small RNA pathway in Aedes and Culex provides immunity to pathogenic mosquito viruses. Our aim here is to harness this response so it provides mosquitoes immunity to human pathogenic viruses transmitted by mosquitoes. For Lepidoptera we develop mutants with easily scored phenotypes. In addition we use CRISPR mutagenesis to explore some aspects of insecticide resistance. For the Hemiptera we are developing genetic methods in order to identify genetic and biochemical pathways that will form the basis of new pest control strategies.
What are the specific objectives of the work with biohazardous materials?
We seek to genetically alter pest insect species in order to control them and the damage they exact upon human welfare either through vectoring human pathogens or by negatively impacting agriculture. To do this we employ transpositional recombination, site-specific recombination and CRISPR/Cas-mediated genome editing, each of which can generate genetically modified insects. Specifically, weseek to develop mosquitoes that are incapable of transmitting human disease. To do this we need to develop genetic strategies for mosquitoes that can deliver suitable genes that can then spread through mosquito populations. We seek to develop Lepidoptera pests that are tagged with new and novel screenable genetic markers and have desired mutations in genes involved in insecticide resistance and in host plant feeding. We seek to develop Hemiptera pests that: are tagged with new and novel screenable genetic markers; have mutations which affect their ability to feed on planet hosts; are modified to replace wild type populations and in doing so replace field populations with populations carrying desired traits. All approaches with all of the species we study also increase our knowledge of basic science underpinning how these species maintain their pestiferous properties.

We will culture this in Aedes albopictus cell culture under BSL2 conditions in my lab in 2237 Genomics and then prepare viral extracts for infection of Aedes aegypti mosquitoes through blood feeding of infected bovine blood. This infection, rearing and analysis of infected mosquitoes will be performed under ACL3 conditions in the IQF. Infected and control mosquitoes will be reared inside perspex cages with mesh and sleeves. This ACL3 insectary suite will be renovated in 2018/19. In separate experiments dealing the whitefly Bemisia tabaci, genetically edited whitefly that contain gene drive systems will be reared in the new ACL3 suite, specifically in cages in the greenhouse attached to it. These strains will be created in the Atkinson laboratory in 2237 Genomics and reared as immobile nymphs in the ACL2 insectary within it. They will be transported in double sealed plastic containers to the IQF before emergence.
